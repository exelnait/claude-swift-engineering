# Naming & UX Writing

Naming is as fundamental to the experience as layout, interaction, and visual scheme. It happens at every level — app, menus, tabs, settings, buttons, plans — and all those choices add up to how someone feels about your app. Good names build on each other and become the language of your app.

## The Three Criteria

| Criterion | Question | About |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Belongs** | Does it fit your app and sit well among everything else you've named? | Fit |
| **Sets expectations** | When someone reads it, does it deliver what they predict they'll find? | Clarity & trust |
| **Works everywhere** | Does it hold up across languages, markets, platforms, and contexts? | Reach |

> A name won't always check every box, and you may have your own criteria (trademark, regulations). Treat these as a guide, not a rulebook — the trade-offs are yours. It's great when a name passes all three.

### Clarity vs brand

- Lean to **clarity** when ambiguity is costly. *Apple Cash* labels the amount **Balance** — the industry-standard, well-understood, neutral term. "Spending Power" is ambiguous and can feel like judgment when low; "Current Funds" sounds like a spreadsheet and isn't how people talk. Clarity and trust came first, not brand expression. Sometimes the obvious word is right because it's already doing the job.
- Lean to **brand/creative** when it genuinely fits and you're willing to do the work to make it understood. Gym plans "Lightweight/Heavyweight" can fit the brand but add a learning curve vs "Basic/All Access". Ask what matters most given where the feature lives.

## The Exercise: get to the right name

Start with **who you're building for** — people want to know what a feature does *for them*, not the technology or function.

### 1. Audience → Think / Feel / Do

With a specific audience in mind, brainstorm (one idea per sticky note, don't filter — more ideas are better):

- **Think** — what should people think about this feature? (easy, helpful, clever)
- **Feel** — what should they feel? (the fun of rediscovery; secure, knowing it's private)
- **Do** — what should they do? (find it, use it, share it)

### 2. Group by theme

Step back; different words point to the same feeling. Cluster them. *(Maps "places you've been" → Ease, Excitement, Security.)*

### 3. Generate candidates, cross out misfits

Cross out names that don't fit your app, feel vague, or won't translate.

### 4. The sentence test

Drop each candidate into a sentence you'd actually read or say:

> "Hey, check out **Private Memories**." · "Just search for **Visited Places**."

A name that reads and sounds natural is worth exploring. *(Maps landed on **Visited Places** — descriptive, at home with "places" elsewhere in the UI, sets the right ownership expectation, and translates.)*

### 5. Evaluate against the three criteria

Pick the name that honors your themes and tests well. It doesn't have to pass all three — but it's great when it does.

## Approaches that all work

- **Descriptive** — *Enhance Dialogue* (Podcasts): a verb puts the person in control; it answers *what's enhanced* and *for whom* before you tap, and the same name on Apple TV is more evidence it belongs. (Beats "Vocal Isolation" — an engineering term — and "Clarify Speech" — only half the story.)
- **Emotional** — *Memories* (Photos): an algorithmic grouping, but people seek a memory, not an algorithm. Clarity from emotion, not explanation.
- **Branded / invented** — *AutoMix* (Music): *Auto* (happens automatically) + *Mix* (blends songs) form a new word you understand immediately. Earns clarity from its parts, so the invented word needn't explain itself.

The criteria don't change across approaches — only how you weigh them.

## Checklist

- [ ] Names what the feature does **for the person**, not the tech/function
- [ ] **Belongs** — fits the app and your existing names
- [ ] **Sets the right expectation** — and the tone doesn't pass judgment (esp. finance/health)
- [ ] **Works everywhere** — translates; holds across markets, platforms, contexts (see `localization`)
- [ ] Passes the **sentence test** — natural to read and say
- [ ] Verb form when the feature is something you *do*, not *have*
